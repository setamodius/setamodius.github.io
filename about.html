About

How I got here, and why I keep going.

Early curiosity

My interest in engineering started long before it became a career — taking electronics apart to understand how they worked, then building small circuits and programs just to see what was possible. Microcontrollers and the first lines of code had the same appeal: a system that does exactly what you tell it to, no more and no less, and the satisfaction of figuring out why it doesn't when it refuses to.

From embedded systems to software architecture

That early curiosity turned into a profession working close to the hardware — reading Modbus registers, writing firmware for microcontrollers, debugging signal issues on a bench with an oscilloscope. Over time the scope widened. The same instinct that wanted to understand a single sensor eventually wanted to understand the whole system: how dozens of field devices, communication links, and software services come together into something a control room operator can trust at 3am.

That widening is what pulled me from embedded development into software architecture — designing the platforms that the field devices talk to, not just the devices themselves.

Tunnel automation and intelligent transportation

A large part of my career has been spent on tunnel automation and intelligent transportation systems — ventilation, lighting, traffic control, dynamic message signs, and the SCADA platforms that tie them together. These are unforgiving environments: international deployments, on-site commissioning, hardware that has to keep running with no patience for "it works on my machine." That work taught me more about reliability and operational discipline than any textbook could.

Curiosity about AI and what comes next

The same question that drove me to take apart electronics as a kid — "how does this actually work" — is what draws me to artificial intelligence today. I'm not interested in AI as a buzzword; I'm interested in it as a system, with its own failure modes, its own architecture, its own version of a Modbus register that someone, somewhere, has to understand deeply. I expect it to reshape how industrial software gets built, and I'd rather be the one who understands it than the one who reacts to it.

Philosophy

I believe in simplicity over complexity, in systems that are boring in the best way — predictable, well-understood, and easy to operate at 3am by someone who isn't me. I'd rather spend an extra day removing a dependency than an extra year maintaining it. Continuous improvement isn't a slogan here; it's the actual mechanism by which any of this gets better — one honest postmortem, one refactor, one better-chosen protocol at a time.
